Berger is the book I keep coming back to when I forget what photography is for. It is a book of essays from a television series, but it does not read like either — it reads like someone thinking carefully and out loud, in a way that gives the reader permission to think alongside.

The first essay — the one on reproduction — is the one I have given away most often. I gave away three copies last year. The argument is simple enough to summarise (the meaning of an image changes when it is reproduced and recontextualised) but it is the *way* he gets there, the patient turning of the image like a stone in the hand, that does the work. You cannot really paraphrase Berger. You can only hand the book over.

The chapters on the oil-painting tradition are the ones I underestimated when I was twenty. They were too long, I thought, and the argument too tidy. Returning to them at thirty I find them tender and angry in roughly equal measure, and the tidiness turns out to be a kind of moral exactness. He is not letting anyone off the hook, including himself.

I do not know how many more times I will read this. Probably as many times as I can.
