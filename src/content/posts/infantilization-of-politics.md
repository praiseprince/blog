Politics today is no longer about organizing care into action. It is not about law, structure, or even the messy work of collective responsibility. Increasingly, politics has become a spectacle: a meme, a clickbait headline, a viral video of two ideologies yelling past each other in a YouTube studio.

Platforms like Jubilee claim to make politics accessible. Sometimes that is the stated goal. In practice, a lot of these formats flatten complex issues into easily digestible content. Disagreement becomes entertainment. Political education gets replaced by performance.

This infantilization of politics is not harmless. It trains people to treat politics as identity work instead of social responsibility. The right-wing strategist does not need to believe everything they say. They only need to own the libs. The liberal influencer does not need to change anyone's mind. They only need to collect approval from people who already agree.

No one is listening. Everyone is performing.

## Debate as content

Take the college debate circuit around figures like Charlie Kirk. The point is often not understanding. The point is control. If the format lets one person steamroll a student with rhetorical tricks and selective data, the clip becomes the proof. The argument does not have to be serious. It only has to look dominant in a thirty-second video.

Even when the other side is more prepared, the structure still rewards performance. A debate can look like a clash of ideas while everyone involved is really playing for clips, applause, and the feeling of victory.

The problem is not that debates are always bad. When I was younger, watching politicians argue taught me to look for cracks in their logic. Even bad debates can reveal what a person is avoiding. But a lot of debate content now places unqualified ideologues, influencers, students, and professional performers into the same frame and pretends that the result is political education.

It usually is not clarity. It is noise.

## Cheap certainty

The internet has made knowledge accessible, but it has also made certainty cheap. Anyone can build a worldview from TikToks, YouTube essays, Reddit threads, and clipped debates. There is value in making information easier to find, but there is also a real danger when partial knowledge starts feeling like expertise.

Political actors exploit this. They do not spread misinformation only because they are stupid. They do it because it works. It polarizes. It monetizes. It mobilizes. It is not a mistake. It is a model.

Liberal media is not innocent here either. Its obsession with outrage means it often mirrors the same tactics while claiming the moral high ground. Politics becomes a game of dunking, destroying, and decimating. What gets lost is humility, curiosity, and any real effort to understand what conditions produced the argument in the first place.

## When tragedy becomes a format

The memification of political events is another symptom. I see people post memes about war, injustice, and humanitarian crises with the same tone they use for a bad football match. The post is not always empathetic or informed. Sometimes it is just a way of saying, "I saw this. I am in the loop."

But politics is not sports commentary. It is not only about knowing what happened. It is about remembering that real people are dying, suffering, being displaced, losing homes, losing families, losing futures.

When a meme about Gaza or a political assassination gets more attention than the event itself, something is deeply broken. Politics should never have been reduced to content. It should be about people. It should be about care.

We are collapsing into a culture where nothing is sacred, not because we are rebellious, but because we are numb.

Alan Turing is an example I keep coming back to. The man whose ideas sit underneath modern computation was chemically castrated by the British government for being gay. He helped defeat the Nazis and was still punished by the country he served. The state later pardoned him, as if he had committed a crime. That was not an apology. It was an insult wrapped in civility.

Why bring up something from decades ago? Because that is the point. If a society could do that to one of its brightest minds, what does it do to ordinary people when nobody is watching? If progress only changes its aesthetics but not its principles, then the performance of care becomes a substitute for care itself.

## Local responsibility

The solution is not simply more data or better debate formats. The solution has to include returning politics to the local level, where people actually live, suffer, and care. Local government is where abstract law turns into housing, transit, addiction services, education, food, policing, zoning, and daily life.

In Canada, for example, a lot of anger around international students gets directed only at the federal government. But part of the issue begins provincially, especially in Ontario, where education has been turned into a business through aggressive and often exploitative use of DLI permits. If you do not understand the provincial-to-federal pipeline, you end up with a simple story that feels satisfying and explains very little.

Doug Ford was re-elected even though provincial choices directly shaped parts of that crisis. Why? Because most people are not taught to distinguish between levels of government. They often do not know who controls what. It is easier to blame whoever is trending, whoever a subreddit is dunking on, or whichever immigrant group feels safest to scapegoat.

Political infantilization is not only imposed from above. It is often chosen from below, because ignorance feels easier than effort.

## Law still needs trust

Local politics also restores a kind of subjectivity that national politics erases. When you see how laws affect your neighbors, your classmates, your coworkers, and your own street, you understand that law is not sacred because it is written down. It matters because people trust it enough to organize their lives around it.

That trust has to be earned. Too often, we treat laws like divine scripture. But laws are only as powerful as the people who believe in them. When trust erodes, the system follows.

Consensus matters, but not all consensus is virtuous. A local majority can still be cruel, exclusionary, racist, homophobic, or simply wrong. That is why empathy has to be paired with constitutional safeguards and human rights. Care is not the same thing as comfort.

## Care, not content

Politics should not be about who can shout the loudest or manipulate data the best. It should be about care, action, and empathy. Yes, we need national policy and global cooperation. But without local engagement, we are building castles in the air.

Humility is part of the answer, but humility only works when people argue in good faith. When one side treats politics as identity and the other treats it as responsibility, facts alone will not save the conversation.

We need to stop mistaking visibility for validity and spectacle for substance.

Politics is not broken because of bad debates. It is broken because we stopped feeling it. We outsourced political thought to platforms and pundits and forgot that politics is how people live together.

Maybe I started this angry at spectacle debates and fake performances. But the deeper truth is simpler: the problem is not only how we talk about politics. It is how little we feel it.

If we want better politics, we do not need louder voices. We need deeper listening. And that starts where people live, not online, not in a studio, but in the spaces we actually share.
