The first look is automatic. It is what culture and grammar and exhaustion have already prepared for me — a recognition, neat and quickly catalogued. I see *man at a bus stop* or *moth on a windowsill* and the seeing is already finished before I have done anything. The second look is harder. It begins by admitting that the first was a lie of convenience.

Most of what I want from a life of attention is hidden between those two glances. The catalogue is what I have inherited. The second look is what I am trying to make. Painters and physicists, I think, share this — they both have to be willing to spend a slightly indecent amount of time staring at the same thing.

## On photography as a slowing-down

A point-and-shoot camera, the kind that fits in a back pocket, is not a serious camera. This is its great virtue. It commits to nothing; it has no opinions about depth of field; its little flash is honestly embarrassing. Because it cannot make a great photograph by itself, you must learn to make one through it, and the learning happens almost entirely in the second look.

> To photograph is to hold your breath when all faculties converge in the face of fleeing reality.
> <cite>— Henri Cartier-Bresson</cite>

The first time I read that line, I underlined it like a student. The second time, much later, I noticed that he said *fleeing*, not *moving*. Reality flees. It is not just going somewhere — it is going away. The camera is not a way of stopping that; it is a way of acknowledging it.

## The engineer's version

I think a great deal about how this same discipline shows up in code. A bug, before you understand it, is a story: it looks like the framework's fault, the user's fault, an edge case that "shouldn't really happen." The first look is always exonerating. The second look — the one where you actually `printf` the variable instead of guessing — is humbling, and almost always the correct one.

Bachelard wrote that the scientific spirit is built against the immediate. I have not read enough Bachelard to defend this in a seminar, but the sentence has been useful to me. The immediate is what the eye delivers without effort. Everything interesting requires you to spend an extra second refusing it.

## A small inventory of second looks

I keep a list, on the last page of a notebook, of things I almost missed. It is not romantic. Most entries are dull: *the way the laundry rope sagged before it rained,* or *that the bookshop owner only smiled when he turned away.* The list is useful only because the act of writing it makes a third look possible — the look that is no longer about the thing itself, but about my own habit of looking.

A photograph, for me, is the same kind of entry. It is not the picture that matters. It is the slowing-down that produced it, the second when I refused to walk past.

I worry, sometimes, that the internet is mostly first looks. It rewards them. It is built to. But I think a website — a small one, a personal one — can be a place where the second look is allowed to take its time. Where a photograph can sit on a page for a week, unbothered, until somebody (maybe me) looks at it twice.
